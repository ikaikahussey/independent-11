Stubborn Boogie
with Jamie Winpenny

The Hawaii Independent’s Downtown editor is currently reconnecting with his Irish heritage on his honeymoon throughout Ireland. He’s sending us back the details. Read part 1 here. Keep checking back for more musings from a Hawaii boy in Ireland.

We left Dublin on Wednesday, headed south for Waterford. I’m happy to report that I’m more at ease as a passenger now, although I’ve admittedly missed a lot of the Irish countryside due to the fact that my eyes have been closed much of the way in order to keep my heart in my chest.

We did, however, manage to get briefly lost—it is here that I will reiterate that Wifey is driving. With the help of a teen-aged bogtrotter (think hillbilly), we were soon righted and back on course to Glendalough, the preposterously tranquil site of a monastery dating back over 1500 years. After a long and well marked and maintained walk along the lower lake, we took in the ruins and gravestones of Saint Kevin’s. Along with busloads of geriatric tourists from, we would learn, Indiana. Talk about a non sequitir. But the import and impact of the site was more than palpable.

We rallied for Waterford Castle further south, where we hustled to the golf course for a round on the island that is home to what is actually more like a country manor—at those prices, we could afford to be semantic. It hailed on us on the first hole before clearing into a beautiful evening. When was the last time you finished a round of golf at 9:00 p.m.? Supper was grand.

We lit out for Cork after a brilliant Irish breakfast the next day, stopping for lunch at Reddy’s in Carlow, a pub founded in 1768. The bar matron was obviously there for the ribbon-cutting, and although the service was slow, the food was good and reasonably priced.

We checked in to our hotel Cork and popped across the street for a bite at Shelbourne’s, where I had what is quite possibly the greatest food item I’ve ever had in my life: a bacon and cheese bap (sounds like “bape”). I also had my first Beamish Stout Ale, possibly the greatest beverage I’ve ever had in my life. This after innumerable Guiness pints in the land from which they hail.

We met lad from Cork at Shelbourne’s, Corry, who confided that there is no way to understand cricket if you’re not born with a bat in your hand and “a wicket up your arse.” He was more loose and friendly than you’d expect from a well-traveled electrical engineer for Apple. He proved to be our man, taking us to an Anna Banana’s clone and then a blues bar where the stellar band was playing for a children’s literacy charity. Wifey peeled off for the hotel, and Corry took me to a bar full of what appeared to be murderous Russians gangsters. I was rattled and made my own way back to the hotel by cab.

“How are ye finding Ireland, then, lad?” asked the driver.

“I’m loving how green it is,” I said. “I can’t imagine the sheep ever going hungry here.”

“Yeah,” he laughed. “Or lonely!”
